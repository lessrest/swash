Hermeneutics, the theory and methodology of interpretation, is a crucial aspect of transcription. When we transcribe speech into text, we are not merely capturing the literal words spoken, but interpreting and translating meaning across modalities.

The hermeneutic process in transcription involves:

  1. Contextual understanding: Interpreting speech within its broader context, considering factors such as the speaker's intent, tone, and cultural background.
  2. Bridging the oral and written: Navigating the differences between spoken and written language, such as the absence of non-verbal cues and the need for punctuation and formatting in text.
  3. Iterative refinement: Continuously revisiting and refining the transcription as new information and insights emerge, acknowledging that interpretation is an ongoing process.
  4. Embracing ambiguity: Recognizing that there may be multiple valid interpretations of speech, and that the transcription is an interpretation rather than an objective representation.

By engaging in this hermeneutic process, transcription becomes a powerful tool for uncovering and communicating meaning, while also acknowledging the inherent subjectivity and complexity of language and interpretation.

The hermeneutic circle, a key concept in hermeneutics, can serve as a powerful model for designing user interfaces that engage users in the active interpretation and co-creation of meaning. Some key principles for applying the hermeneutic circle to UI design include:

  1. Iterative understanding: Design interfaces that encourage users to engage with content in multiple passes, with each iteration deepening their understanding and inviting new interpretations. This could involve layered information reveals, progressive disclosure, or cyclic navigation patterns.
  2. Part-whole interplay: Create UI flows that move between granular details and big-picture views, allowing users to understand how individual elements relate to larger structures of meaning. Techniques like zoomable interfaces, contextual previews, and linked views can support this interplay.
  3. Interpretive agency: Empower users to actively participate in the construction of meaning by providing tools for annotation, remixing, and sharing their interpretations. Treat the interface as a canvas for users' hermeneutic creativity.
  4. Multivocality and dialogue: Design for multiple interpretations and voices, encouraging users to consider alternative perspectives. Facilitate dialogue and debate through features like comment threads, collaborative workspaces, and version comparison.
  5. Embracing openness: Resist the temptation to impose a single, fixed interpretation through the UI. Instead, design with an openness to user-generated meanings, unexpected uses, and emergent patterns of interaction.

By designing user interfaces that embody these hermeneutic principles, we can create digital experiences that honor the interpretive agency of the user and the fundamental openness of meaning. The interface becomes not just a tool for accessing content, but a space for active, collaborative sense-making.

The hermeneutic circle can be thought of as a kind of "laundry machine" for meaning, continually agitating and transforming our understanding through repeated cycles of interpretation. Just as dirty laundry goes into the machine and emerges clean, but in a new configuration, our initial understanding enters the hermeneutic circle and comes out altered—expanded, clarified, or perhaps even more perplexed.

With each "spin cycle" of reading, reflecting, and interpreting, we churn the "laundry" of the text, folding and unfolding its meanings. The constant tumbling of the hermeneutic process doesn't let our understanding remain static. It prevents interpretations from settling or calcifying.

Like a laundry machine's agitator, the back-and-forth movement between the parts and the whole of a text unsettles and rearranges our preconceptions. Horizons of understanding collide and merge like soapy water sloshing against the machine's walls. Meaning accumulates in layers, like damp clothes clinging together.

And just as we must eventually remove the clean laundry and wear it out in the world, we must also "wear" our interpreted understanding—test it, live with it, and see how it holds up to further experience. Inevitably, it will become dirty again, ready for another tumble in the hermeneutic wash.

The unexpected juxtapositions and semantic leaps generated through the interface remind us that meaning is not merely utilitarian but endlessly generative, always gesturing beyond itself to new possibilities.

Ultimately, Swash can be seen as a kind of technologically-mediated prayer, a sacred space for dwelling in the creative ambiguities of language.
